"Pi", what is it?


Pi (π), usually expressed as the lowercase form of the sixteenth letter of the Greek alphabet, is the mathemtical constant which represents the ratio of the circumference of a circle over its diameter. This mathematical constant appears all throughout circles regardless of shape or size, always giving the same value of 3.14..., when dividing the circumference of a circle over its diameter. Pi is known to be one of the most famous mathematical constants, being introduced to third graders or primary 3 students.

History of Pi


Ancient Times (BCE)

During this period, pi was discovered with its approximations only being as close to knowing 1 or 2 decimal places. Pi was actually discovered 4000 years ago with proof of ancient tablets from 1900BC to 1600BC by Egypt and Babylon. Tablets are to have shown for it to be approximately equal to 3 or 3.125 by the Babylons and equal to 3.1605 for Egypt in a slightly younger tablet.


Polygon Approximation

The first recorded proof of rigorous calculation of pi was from Archimedes. He found the area of a polygon of a size bigger than a unit circle and that of a polygon smaller than a unit circle and then used its two values to find a range of which pi may lie, giving a range of pi being 3.1408 to 3.1429. It is believed Archimedes is the cause of the popular approximation of pi to be 22/7 In the years to have come, many mathematicians from ancient China and Greek have used a similar method, managing to make the approximation as close as 9 decimal places, with Zu Chongzi having used a regular 24-576 gon and done lengthy calculations to discover pi up to 9 decimal places.


Pre-modern Era

Up to the 17th century, the most digits to have been discovered was around 38 digits. Until the discovery of approximating pi through using a infinite series, summing terms infinitely to get an exact value of pi. Starting from the 16th century, mathematicians discovered infinite series formulas that could get the exact values of pi. Starting in 1699, the number of digits discovered jumped up to 71 digits, shattering the previous record of 39 by two times using the old polygon method. From this period of time, many mathematicians had formulated infinite series to converge to pi as fast as possible with around 707 digits discovered by 1873. Not only that, pi was discovered to be irrational by Swiss scientist Johann Heinrich Lambert in 1768 and transcendental by Ferdinand von Lindemann in 1882. Euler popularised the usage of pi being 3.14, with his work being popular and Euler being widely known throughout Europe.
